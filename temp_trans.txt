And every single one of them is someone who told me at some point that they couldn't find flexible work that respected their background, their language, their hours. So that's where I want to start — not with the product, with the people.

Rania: Tell me more about the 200. What does that actually look like on the ground?

Dana: So when I started YOUMNA — يمنـى — the whole premise was: there are incredibly talented Arab women, many of them bilingual, many of them with degrees, who are being completely overlooked because the global remote work market doesn't know how to find them and doesn't know how to retain them. The mismatch isn't skills. It's visibility and trust.

Rania: And the 200 placements — what's the spread? Industries, countries?

Dana: UAE, Saudi, Egypt, Jordan mostly. Industries — heavy on fintech, a lot of executive assistance for founders, some legal, some marketing. What surprised me is how many placements ended up being with MENA-based startups, not international companies. The ecosystem is starting to hire from within itself, which is a huge shift from even eighteen months ago.

[02:15]
Rania: That's actually a really interesting signal for the MENA startup ecosystem angle. Is that worth a post on its own?

Dana: Absolutely. Because the narrative for years has been: Arab talent looks West. Get the foreign job, get the dollar salary, prove yourself to someone outside the region. And what we're seeing with YOUMNA placements is the opposite. Founders here — in Dubai, in Riyadh, in Cairo — are actively choosing to hire people who understand the culture, who can work across Arabic and English without losing nuance, who don't need a briefing on Ramadan schedules or how decisions get made in family-owned businesses.

Rania: You said nuance — can you be specific about what gets lost in translation?

Dana: Here's a real example. One of our YOUMNA — that's what we call the women we place, our right hands — she was supporting a fintech founder in Abu Dhabi. He had a board meeting coming up with a government entity. She knew — without being told — that you never open with the deck. You open with tea, with asking about the family, with letting the relationship land before the numbers. His previous EA, who was excellent by every international standard, had tried to run it like a London board meeting and it had cost him the room. Culture is operational. It is not soft skills.

[04:30]
Rania: I love that framing — culture is operational. That could be a hook.

Dana: Yes. And this connects to the fintech angle I want to push this quarter. I spent five years at Visa running digital banking across MENA. What I learned is that financial products fail in this region not because of technology but because of trust infrastructure. People don't trust anonymous platforms. They trust people they know, institutions with local presence, products that speak Arabic — and I mean genuinely speak Arabic, not Google Translate Arabic.

Rania: Is YOUMNA touching fintech clients directly now?

Dana: We have seven fintech placements this quarter. Four are with neobanks trying to expand into the Gulf. They hire our YOUMNA specifically because she bridges the gap — she's the human trust layer in a market that still runs on relationship. One of our clients told me last month: "Dana, your person is worth three product managers to me because she can walk into a majlis and come back with a decision." That's the ROI we're selling. Not tasks completed. Decisions unlocked.

[06:10]
Rania: Let's talk about the delegation angle. That's been performing really well for you in terms of engagement.

Dana: Because every founder I know is drowning. And the conversation about delegation in the Arab world is complicated by ego and by culture. There's still this idea — especially among male founders, I'll say it — that having help means you can't handle it. That asking for a right hand is weakness. I want to dismantle that completely. The most powerful people I know delegate everything that is not their genius.

Rania: How do you talk about your own delegation?

Dana: Personally? My YOUMNA — her name is Lara — she has been with me for fourteen months. She runs my calendar, she manages founder outreach, she knows which messages I need to see immediately and which I don't. Because of Lara, I spent forty hours last quarter on product instead of inbox. Forty hours. That's the equivalent of a full work week that went into building something real instead of managing noise. And I want every founder to have that. That's the whole company.

Rania: That's a post. That's literally a post right there.

Dana: Write it down. I want to say: I didn't build YOUMNA because I saw a market opportunity. I built it because I needed a Lara and I couldn't find one. And then I realized every Arab female founder I knew needed one too.

[08:00]
Rania: Last angle — the women in tech piece. You've been a bit quiet on that personally. Why?

Dana: Because I don't want to be the person who posts about women in tech without saying something real. The data on Arab women in the workforce is devastating if you look honestly at it. Participation rates, leadership representation, the salary gap — it's there. But I think what's missing from the conversation isn't more statistics. It's more names. More faces. More stories that say: she did it here, in this region, with this specific set of constraints, and it worked.

Rania: So you want to center other YOUMNA stories, not just the aggregate numbers?

Dana: Exactly. Last month we placed a woman in Amman — Hana — who had been out of the workforce for six years raising her children. Two weeks into her placement she told me: "Dana, I forgot I was good at this." I forgot I was good at this. How do you put a market size on that? How do you put that in a pitch deck? You can't. But you can tell her story. And her story is the thesis.

Rania: That's your next post.

Dana: That's my next ten posts. We have two hundred Hanas. We just have to stop hiding them behind aggregate data and start letting them speak.
